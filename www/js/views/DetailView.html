Queen/Worker: Queen has buff tail. Worker' tail is largely white, usually with a hint of buff at the front margin. Single yellow stripe on front of the thorax, one on the abdomen. Yellow bands slightly darker/dirtier than in the White-tailed bumblebee.

Male: As female, usually has buff tail.

Similar species:
- White-tailed bumblebee: Male and queen have white tail. Worker is very hard to separate.

- Bilberry bumblebee: Yellow bands are brighter. More red in the tail.

A common bee, often nesting under garden sheds.

Queen/Worker: Pure white tail. One lemon yellow band on front of the thorax and one on the abdomen.

Male: As female, but has yellow face. Has one more yellow band at the bottom of the thorax.

Similar species:
- Buff-tailed bumblebee: Male and queen have buff tails. Worker is very hard to separate.

Very common on chalk downland, frequent in gardens.

Queen/Worker: Black bee with orange-red tail, which is less than 50% of length of abdomen.

Male: As female, but has yellow face, and yellow bands on the thorax.

Similar species:
- Red-shanked carder bee: Orange hairs of pollen basket on hind legs, not black.
- Red-tailed cuckoo bumblebee: Smoky wings, no pollen basket, abdomen has much less hair.

Queen/Worker: A very long-tongued species preferring deep flowers (e.g. foxgloves, Delphinium, honeysuckle) with a white tail and yellow bands, including one at the rear of the thorax.

Male: As female.

Similar species:
- Heath bumblebee: Much shorter face.
- Ruderal bumblebee: Not easy to separate. All-black specimens are more likely to be Ruderal bumblebee. Front yellow band on the thorax is wider than the back one in Garden bumblebee.
